Your export services remain zero-rated under GST, meaning you don&#8217;t charge tax to foreign clients, but the banks and forex providers do charge 18% GST on their conversion services.</p>



<p>The good news? This GST is calculated on a small service value using government-prescribed methods, making the effective tax rate much lower than you might expect. <h2>What is GST on Foreign Exchange?</h2>



<p>Let&#8217;s start with the basics. GST on foreign exchange refers to the Goods and Services Tax applied when you convert one currency to another or when a bank or authorised dealer facilitates your foreign currency transactions. Introduced in 2017 as part of India&#8217;s unified tax system, GST replaced the earlier service tax regime that governed forex transactions.</p>



<p>Here&#8217;s the critical distinction many business owners miss: <strong>GST is not charged on the foreign currency itself</strong>. Instead, it&#8217;s levied on the service value, the fee charged by banks or forex providers for facilitating the conversion or transaction.</p>



<p>Think of it this way: when your US client sends you $5,000, the money itself isn&#8217;t taxed under GST. But when your bank converts that $5,000 to rupees, they charge a service fee for that conversion, and 18% GST applies to that service fee.</p>



<h3>A Brief History</h3>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="GST exepmtions" class="wp-image-9504" height="1704" src="https://decentro.tech/blog/wp-content/uploads/GST_On_Foreign_Exchange_Exceptions.jpg" width="2500" /></figure>



<p>Before GST was implemented on July 1, 2017, foreign exchange transactions attracted service tax at varying rates. The system was fragmented, with different states applying different rules, creating compliance nightmares for businesses operating across India.</p>



<p>GST brought uniformity. Under the current framework, foreign exchange services are classified as taxable supplies, with clear rules defined under Rule 32 of the CGST Rules, 2017. This standardisation has made it easier for businesses to understand their tax obligations, though the devil remains in the details.</p>



<h2>How GST Works for Export Services</h2>



<p>If you&#8217;re providing services to clients outside India, whether you&#8217;re a developer building apps, a designer creating websites, or a consultant offering expertise, your export transactions receive favourable treatment under GST.</p>



<h3>Zero-Rated Supply Status</h3>



<p>Export of services qualifies as a <strong>zero-rated supply</strong> under Section 16 of the IGST Act. This means you charge 0% GST on your invoices to foreign clients. But zero-rated doesn&#8217;t mean exempt, it&#8217;s actually better. With zero-rated supplies, you can still claim Input Tax Credit (ITC) on GST paid for business expenses like software subscriptions, office rent, or equipment purchases.</p>



<p>To qualify for zero-rated status, your service must meet five conditions:</p>



<ol><li>The supplier (you) must be located in India</li><li>The recipient (your client) must be located outside India</li><li>The place of supply must be outside India</li><li>Payment must be received in convertible foreign currency</li><li>The supplier and recipient cannot be merely branches of the same legal entity</li></ol>



<h3>The Letter of Undertaking (LUT)</h3>



<p>Here&#8217;s where it gets practical. To export services without paying IGST upfront, you need to file a Letter of Undertaking (Form RFD-11) on the GST portal. This simple document, valid for one financial year, allows you to send zero-rated invoices to clients without blocking your cash flow.</p>



<p>The alternative? You&#8217;d have to pay IGST on your export invoices and then claim a refund—a process that can take months and seriously impact working capital. Most businesses file their LUT at the start of each financial year (April) and forget about it until the next renewal.</p>



<h2>Understanding GST Calculation on Forex Conversions</h2>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="GST on currency exchange" class="wp-image-9505" height="1737" src="https://decentro.tech/blog/wp-content/uploads/GST_On_Foreign_Exchange_Currency_Exchange.jpg" width="2500" /></figure>



<p>Now we get to the numbers. When you convert foreign currency to INR, banks and authorised dealers use one of two methods prescribed under Rule 32 of the CGST Rules to calculate the taxable service value.</p>



<h3>Method 1: Calculation-Based Method</h3>



<p>This method applies when one currency is INR and the RBI publishes a reference rate for the foreign currency.</p>



<p><strong>How it works:</strong> The taxable value equals the difference between the transaction rate and the RBI reference rate, multiplied by the amount of currency exchanged.</p>



<p><strong>Example:</strong> You receive $10,000 from a client. Your bank offers an exchange rate of ₹84.50 per dollar, while the RBI reference rate for that day is ₹84.00.</p>



<ul><li>Difference: ₹84.50 &#8211; ₹84.00 = ₹0.50</li><li>Service value: ₹0.50 × 10,000 = ₹5,000</li><li>GST at 18%: ₹900</li></ul>



<p>So even though you&#8217;re converting $10,000 (₹8,45,000), the GST charged is only ₹900—barely 0.1% of the total amount.</p>



<p><strong>When the RBI rate is unavailable:</strong> For currencies where the RBI doesn&#8217;t publish rates, the service value is calculated as 1% of the gross INR amount received or paid.</p>



<h3>Method 2: Slab-Based Method</h3>



<p>This method uses fixed slabs based on the INR value of the transaction:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-table"><table><tbody><tr><td><strong>Transaction Amount (INR)</strong></td><td><strong>Service Value Calculation</strong></td></tr><tr><td>Up to ₹1,00,000</td><td>1% of the amount (minimum ₹250)</td></tr><tr><td>₹1,00,001 to ₹10,00,000</td><td>₹1,000 + 0.5% of amount above ₹1 lakh</td></tr><tr><td>Above ₹10,00,000</td><td>₹5,500 + 0.1% of amount above ₹10 lakh (max ₹60,000)</td></tr></tbody></table></figure>



<p><strong>Example:</strong> You convert ₹5,00,000 worth of foreign currency.</p>



<ul><li>Service value: ₹1,000 + (0.5% of ₹4,00,000)</li><li>Service value: ₹1,000 + ₹2,000 = ₹3,000</li><li>GST at 18%: ₹540</li></ul>



<p>Banks must choose one method and stick with it for the entire financial year, ensuring consistency in how they calculate your charges.</p>



<h2>Latest Updates and Rules (2025-2026)</h2>

<p>The GST landscape continues to evolve. While the core structure for foreign exchange transactions remains stable, 2025 has brought significant reforms to the broader GST system.</p>



<h3>GST 2.0 Reforms</h3>



<p>In September 2025, India launched its most significant GST reform since 2017. The 56th GST Council meeting simplified the tax structure from four slabs (5%, 12%, 18%, 28%) to primarily two slabs (5% and 18%), with a new 40% rate for luxury and sin goods.</p>



<p><strong>What this means for you:</strong> While foreign exchange services continue to attract 18% GST, the overall simplification makes compliance easier. With fewer slabs to manage, businesses can focus on core operations rather than wrestling with classification disputes.</p>



<h3>Enhanced Compliance Measures</h3>



<p>Starting in 2025, several compliance improvements rolled out:</p>



<ul><li><strong>Faster refunds:</strong> Eligible businesses now receive 90% of claimed refunds as provisional refunds, subject to system-driven risk evaluation</li><li><strong>Biometric authentication:</strong> Company directors must complete biometric verification at GST Suvidha Kendras for new registrations</li><li><strong>GSTAT operationalisation:</strong> The GST Appellate Tribunal provides faster dispute resolution, with hearings set to commence by December 2025</li></ul>



<h3>Digital Documentation</h3>



<p>The shift toward digital documentation accelerated in recent years. Physical FIRCs (Foreign Inward Remittance Certificates) have largely been replaced by:</p>



<ul><li><strong>FIRA (Foreign Inward Remittance Advice):</strong> Digital proof of receiving foreign payments</li><li><strong>e-BRC (Electronic Bank Realisation Certificate):</strong> Available through the DGFT portal, linking payments to specific export invoices</li><li><strong>e-FIRC:</strong> Digital certificates issued by banks for inward remittances</li></ul>



<p>These documents serve as crucial proof when claiming GST refunds or demonstrating compliance during audits.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Common Mistakes to Avoid" class="wp-image-9507" height="1158" src="https://decentro.tech/blog/wp-content/uploads/GST_On_Foreign_Exchange_Common_Mistakes.jpg" width="2500" /></figure>



<p>After working with hundreds of businesses, we&#8217;ve seen these errors repeatedly:</p>



<h3>1. Charging GST to Foreign Clients</h3>



<p>Many freelancers and startups mistakenly add 18% GST to invoices for international clients. This is wrong. Export services are zero-rated. Your invoice should clearly state &#8220;Export of Service , GST not applicable (Zero-rated under IGST).&#8221;</p>



<h3>2. Skipping the LUT Filing</h3>



<p>Forgetting to file your LUT means paying IGST upfront on every export invoice. File it in April, mark your calendar for next year, and save yourself the cash flow headache.</p>



<h3>3. Poor Documentation</h3>



<p>Not maintaining proper records of forex transactions, FIRCs, and bank statements creates problems during audits. Keep organised files, digital copies work best, for at least 6 years.</p>



<h3>4. Using Unauthorised Channels</h3>



<p>Some businesses use informal channels or non-RBI-authorised platforms to save on fees. This not only violates FEMA regulations but also means you can&#8217;t claim legitimate GST refunds because you lack proper documentation.</p>



<h3>5. Not Reporting Zero-Rated Exports</h3>



<p>Zero-rated doesn&#8217;t mean unreported. You must still declare your export income in GSTR-1 (Table 6A) and GSTR-3B, even though the tax rate is 0%.</p>

<h1 class="alignfull has-text-align-center">Frequently Asked Questions</h1>



<p><strong>1. Do I need to charge GST to my international clients?</strong></p>



<p>No. Export of services is zero-rated under Section 16 of the IGST Act, meaning you charge 0% GST to foreign clients. Your invoice should clearly state &#8220;Export of services &#8211; Zero-rated supply under GST&#8221; or &#8220;Export of services without payment of IGST under LUT&#8221;. Never add 18% GST to international invoices—this is one of the most common and costly mistakes.</p>



<p><strong>2. Is the LUT valid for multiple clients and transactions?</strong></p>



<p>Yes. A single LUT covers all your export transactions for the entire financial year (April to March), regardless of the number of clients or transaction volume. You don&#8217;t need separate LUTs for each client or invoice. Just file Form RFD-11 once at the start of the financial year and you&#8217;re covered until March 31st. Remember to renew it every April.</p>



<p><strong>3. What&#8217;s the difference between zero-rated and exempt supplies?</strong></p>



<p>Both result in 0% GST to customers, but they&#8217;re fundamentally different for your business. <strong>Zero-rated</strong> (exports): you can claim Input Tax Credit (ITC) on business expenses like software, equipment, and rent. <strong>Exempt</strong> (like education or healthcare): you cannot claim ITC. For exporters, zero-rated status is far more beneficial as it allows you to recover GST paid on inputs, improving your margins.</p>



<p><strong>4. How is GST calculated on forex conversion, and can I negotiate it?</strong></p>



<p>GST is calculated on the service value using Rule 32 methods: either the spread between bank rate and RBI reference rate, or a slab-based formula (1% up to ₹1L, 0.5% from ₹1L-₹10L, 0.1% above ₹10L with ₹60K cap). The GST itself (18%) is non-negotiable, but you can choose forex providers with better exchange rates and lower spreads. Digital payment platforms often offer rates 2-4% better than traditional banks, reducing the base on which GST applies.</p>



<p><strong>5. What documents do I need for GST compliance on foreign payments?</strong></p>



<p>Essential documents include: FIRA/e-FIRC (proof of foreign receipt), export invoices marked zero-rated, bank statements showing forex conversion, LUT filing acknowledgment (Form RFD-11), GST returns (GSTR-1 Table 6A showing exports, GSTR-3B), and contracts/purchase orders from foreign clients. Keep all documents for minimum 6 years. Digital copies are acceptable and recommended for easier retrieval during audits or refund claims.</p>
