<h2>Why This Matters Right Now</h2>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="statistics related to Aadhaar" class="wp-image-9667" height="1427" src="https://decentro.tech/blog/wp-content/uploads/OVSE-TofuBlog_Facts.jpg" width="2500" /></figure>



<p>India&#8217;s digital identity infrastructure is one of the most advanced in the world. Over 141.88 crore Aadhaar IDs have been generated, and Aadhaar-based e-KYC transactions crossed 2,393 crore cumulatively by April 2025,&nbsp; growing nearly 40% year-on-year.</p>



<p>For businesses, this isn&#8217;t just a statistic. It reflects the scale at which customer onboarding is now happening digitally. Banks, NBFCs, fintechs, insurers, and even logistics platforms are using Aadhaar-based verification to reduce drop-offs, eliminate paperwork, and stay compliant.</p>



<p>But here&#8217;s what many founders miss: you can&#8217;t just use Aadhaar Offline KYC without a formal registration. That registration is called OVSE status, and understanding the process is the first step to going live legally and quickly.</p>



<h2>What is an OVSE?</h2>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Evolution of Offline Aadhaar" class="wp-image-9668" height="2812" src="https://decentro.tech/blog/wp-content/uploads/24.04.2026-2.jpg" width="2250" /></figure>



<p>OVSE stands for Offline Verification Seeking Entity. It is a category of organisation formally recognised by the Unique Identification Authority of India (UIDAI) as authorised to verify Aadhaar identity offline.</p>



<p>Unlike online e-KYC (which pulls data directly from UIDAI&#8217;s Central Identities Data Repository in real-time), offline verification works differently. An OVSE validates identity by checking the digital signature embedded in the Aadhaar QR code or in the user-generated encrypted file, without querying the UIDAI database at the time of verification. The process is privacy-first by design: no biometrics are involved, and the full Aadhaar number is never exposed.</p>



<p>Think of it this way: online e-KYC is like calling a government office to confirm someone&#8217;s identity every time you need to verify them. OVSE-based offline KYC is like being given a certified, tamper-proof identity document that you can verify on your end, independently and instantly.</p>



<p>Importantly, becoming an OVSE does not require a licence in the traditional sense. It requires a formal registration with UIDAI, along with compliance commitments, which makes the process more accessible than many founders assume.</p>



<h2>Who Can Become an OVSE?</h2>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Who Can Become an OVSE?" class="wp-image-9669" height="1583" src="https://decentro.tech/blog/wp-content/uploads/OVSE-TofuBlog_Who_can_become.jpg" width="2500" /></figure>



<p>According to UIDAI guidelines, any public, private, or non-governmental organisation is eligible to apply for OVSE status, provided it has a clear, lawful, and reasonable purpose for conducting Aadhaar-based offline verification.</p>



<p>Eligible entity types include:</p>



<ul><li>Banks, NBFCs, and microfinance companies</li><li>Insurance companies and brokers</li><li>Telecom operators</li><li>Digital lending and fintech platforms</li><li>HR, staffing, and background verification companies</li><li>EdTech platforms and universities</li><li>Healthcare providers and hospitals</li><li>Logistics and e-commerce platforms</li></ul>



<p>The operative word is &#8220;purpose.&#8221; UIDAI requires that your reason for needing offline verification is documented, specific, and tied to a genuine business function — such as customer onboarding, fraud reduction, employee verification, or KYC compliance for financial services.</p>



<h2>What Are the Benefits of Becoming an OVSE?</h2>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="What Are the Benefits of Becoming an OVSE?" class="wp-image-9670" height="1583" src="https://decentro.tech/blog/wp-content/uploads/OVSE-TofuBlog_Benefits.jpg" width="2500" /></figure>



<p>For businesses that onboard customers at scale, OVSE status isn&#8217;t just a compliance checkbox — it&#8217;s a meaningful operational and strategic advantage. Here&#8217;s what changes when you&#8217;re registered:</p>



<p><strong>Faster, paperless onboarding.</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>Offline Aadhaar verification eliminates the need to collect, scan, and manually review physical documents. Customers can complete identity verification in minutes, directly from their smartphone, without uploading copies of their Aadhaar card. The result is a dramatically shorter onboarding funnel and lower drop-off rates.</p>



<p><strong>Tamper-proof, legally valid identity verification.</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>Every verification performed through the OVSE framework is backed by UIDAI&#8217;s digital signature. The identity data is cryptographically verified — not self-declared — which significantly reduces fraud risk and gives you an auditable, legally defensible record of every KYC transaction.</p>



<p><strong>No dependency on real-time UIDAI connectivity.</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>Because offline verification doesn&#8217;t require a live query to UIDAI&#8217;s central database, verifications can be completed even in low-connectivity environments. This is particularly relevant for businesses operating across Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities or in field-based settings.</p>



<p><strong>Access to Aadhaar Verifiable Credentials (AVC).</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>One of the most significant recent developments for OVSEs is the introduction of the Aadhaar Verifiable Credential. Introduced via the Aadhaar (Authentication and Offline Verification) Amendment Regulations, 2025, the AVC is designed to ease the KYC verification process for regulated entities.<a href="https://vinodkothari.com/2025/12/a-new-way-to-verify-aadhaar-offline-introduction-of-face-matching/" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" target="_blank"> Vinod Kothari Consultants</a>.</p>



<p> It is a digitally signed document issued by UIDAI to the Aadhaar Number Holder, and may contain the last four digits of the Aadhaar number, demographic data such as name, address, gender, date of birth, and photograph, which the holder can share, in full or in part, with an OVSE for the purpose of verifying their demographic information or photograph.<a href="https://uidai.gov.in/en/1468-uncategorised/webinar-frequently-asked-questions/19603-9-what-is-aadhaar-verifiable-credential-in-the-context-of-aadhaar-app.html"> </a></p>



<p><a href="https://uidai.gov.in/en/1468-uncategorised/webinar-frequently-asked-questions/19603-9-what-is-aadhaar-verifiable-credential-in-the-context-of-aadhaar-app.html" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" target="_blank">UIDAI</a> Only registered OVSEs are authorised to receive and verify AVCs, making OVSE registration a prerequisite for accessing this newer, more privacy-respecting verification pathway.</p>



<p><strong>Built-in user consent and data minimisation.</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>The OVSE framework is structured around explicit consent and the principle of minimal data collection — both of which align closely with India&#8217;s Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA). For businesses preparing for DPDPA compliance, building your onboarding on OVSE infrastructure puts you ahead of the curve, not scrambling to retrofit consent flows later.</p>



<p><strong>Competitive positioning.</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>OVSE status signals to regulators, investors, and enterprise partners that your identity verification practices meet a formal, government-recognised standard. For fintechs seeking RBI partnerships, or platforms building B2B products, this matters.</p>



<p><strong>Reduced operational costs.</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>Manual KYC reviews, physical document storage, and re-verification workflows are expensive. Moving to OVSE-based digital verification reduces the per-customer cost of onboarding significantly, particularly at scale.</p>



<h2>What Do You Need Before Applying?</h2>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="What Do You Need Before Applying?" class="wp-image-9671" height="1121" src="https://decentro.tech/blog/wp-content/uploads/OVSE-TofuBlog_Requirements.jpg" width="2500" /></figure>



<p>Getting your OVSE registration approved starts well before you fill out the application. There are two things UIDAI looks for: a valid stated purpose and the right documentation.</p>



<p><strong>On the purpose side</strong>, you need to clearly articulate why your business needs offline Aadhaar verification. Vague answers won&#8217;t help. Acceptable purposes include:</p>



<ul><li>Onboarding customers for regulated financial products (loans, insurance, accounts)</li><li>Reducing identity fraud during user sign-up</li><li>Verifying employees or gig workers at scale</li><li>KYC for government benefit disbursement</li></ul>



<p><strong>On the documentation side</strong>, the following are required:</p>



<ul><li>Certificate of Incorporation or Registration</li><li>Company profile (describing your business and the nature of identity verification needed)</li><li>Letter of Authorisation (from a senior official or board-level signatory)</li><li>A self-declaration confirming that your organisation will comply with the Aadhaar Act, adhere to UIDAI&#8217;s data governance rules, and use Aadhaar data only for its stated lawful purpose</li></ul>



<p>This last document — the self-declaration — is not a formality. It is a legal commitment. UIDAI takes data misuse seriously, and OVSE status comes with ongoing obligations that your team needs to understand before you go live.</p>



<h2>The Step-by-Step OVSE Registration Process</h2>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="The Step-by-Step OVSE Registration Process" class="wp-image-9672" height="1400" src="https://decentro.tech/blog/wp-content/uploads/OVSE-TofuBlog_Registration_Process.jpg" width="2500" /></figure>



<p>Once your documents are in order, the registration follows four key steps:</p>



<p><strong>Step 1: Submit an Online Application</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>Applications are submitted through the UIDAI portal. You&#8217;ll provide your organisation details, the intended use case for offline verification, and upload the required documents. Be specific and thorough here — incomplete or vague applications are a common reason for delays.</p>



<p><strong>Step 2: Sign the Agreement with UIDAI</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>Once your application is reviewed and accepted, UIDAI will ask your organisation to execute a formal agreement. This agreement defines the scope of your OVSE usage, your data handling responsibilities, and the consequences of non-compliance. It&#8217;s a binding legal document, so loop in your legal team.</p>



<p><strong>Step 3: Complete Technical Integration</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>After the agreement is signed, you&#8217;ll need to integrate with the Aadhaar offline verification system. This involves building the capability to read and validate the digital signature on Aadhaar QR codes or encrypted user files, handle consent workflows, and generate audit logs. More on how to shortcut this step below.</p>



<p><strong>Step 4: Pay the Nominal Registration Fee</strong>&nbsp;</p>



<p>UIDAI charges a nominal fee as part of the registration process. This is not a high cost, but it is part of the formal process and should be factored into your timeline.</p>



<p>The overall process, from application submission to go-live, can take anywhere from a few weeks to a few months, depending on how prepared your documentation is and how quickly the technical integration is completed.</p>



<h2>Compliance Rules You Must Follow as an OVSE</h2>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Compliance Rules You Must Follow as an OVSE" class="wp-image-9673" height="1427" src="https://decentro.tech/blog/wp-content/uploads/OVSE-TofuBlog_Guidelines.jpg" width="2500" /></figure>



<p>Getting registered is step one. Staying compliant is an ongoing commitment. UIDAI has clear operational guidelines for all OVSEs, and violations can result in suspension or cancellation of your status.</p>



<p>Here&#8217;s what you&#8217;re committing to:</p>



<p><strong>Explicit consent before every verification.</strong> </p>



<p>You must obtain informed consent from the user before performing any offline verification. The consent must explain what data is being accessed, why, and how it will be used. The user has the right to decline, and you must have an alternative verification method ready for those who do.</p>



<p><strong>No denial of service based on Aadhaar refusal.</strong> </p>



<p>If a user declines Aadhaar-based verification, your organisation cannot deny them the service entirely. This is a non-negotiable UIDAI rule.</p>



<p><strong>No storage of full Aadhaar numbers.</strong> </p>



<p>UIDAI mandates strict data minimisation. Full Aadhaar numbers must not be stored. If any reference is needed, you must use a masked Aadhaar number or a reference ID.</p>



<p><strong>Data used only for its stated purpose.</strong> </p>



<p>The identity data obtained through offline KYC cannot be repurposed, shared with third parties, or used for anything beyond the specific function declared at registration. Post-verification, data should be deleted unless there is a legally valid reason to retain it.</p>



<p><strong>Security and audit controls.</strong> </p>



<p>You must maintain encrypted storage, tamper-proof audit logs of every verification activity, and conduct periodic internal compliance audits. This is not optional — UIDAI can request audit evidence.</p>



<h2>The Real Challenge: Technical Integration</h2>



<p>The registration process itself is manageable. Where most businesses hit delays is in the technical build.</p>



<p>To operate as a compliant OVSE, you need to be able to:</p>



<ul><li>Parse and validate digitally signed Aadhaar QR codes</li><li>Process encrypted offline files and verify UIDAI signatures</li><li>Build and manage user consent workflows</li><li>Implement Aadhaar number masking and data minimisation protocols</li><li>Maintain tamper-proof audit logs</li></ul>



<p>For an engineering team that&#8217;s never built this before, it&#8217;s a meaningful lift — and getting it wrong isn&#8217;t just a technical problem, it&#8217;s a compliance risk.</p>



<p>This is where many startups lose weeks or months: trying to build the full stack in-house, running into edge cases with signature validation, or discovering that their consent flow doesn&#8217;t meet UIDAI requirements.</p>

<h1 class="alignfull has-text-align-center">Frequently Asked Questions</h1>



<p><strong>1. Is OVSE registration the same as an Aadhaar licence?</strong> </p>



<p>No. OVSE registration is not a licence — it is a formal recognition and agreement with UIDAI that authorises your organisation to perform offline Aadhaar verification. It is more accessible than a full AUA/KUA licence and does not require building a direct connection to UIDAI&#8217;s Central Identities Data Repository.</p>



<p><strong>2. How long does the OVSE registration process take?</strong> </p>



<p>The timeline varies depending on how well-prepared your documentation is and the speed of UIDAI&#8217;s review process. In practice, it can take anywhere from a few weeks to a couple of months from application submission to going live. Having your Certificate of Incorporation, company profile, Letter of Authorisation, and self-declaration ready before you apply can significantly reduce back-and-forth.</p>



<p><strong>3. Can a startup or early-stage company apply for OVSE status?</strong> </p>



<p>Yes. UIDAI&#8217;s eligibility criteria do not specify a minimum company size or revenue threshold. Any registered entity — including a private limited company or LLP — can apply, provided it has a legitimate and clearly stated reason for requiring offline Aadhaar verification.</p>



<p><strong>4. What happens if we fail to comply with UIDAI&#8217;s OVSE guidelines after registration?</strong> </p>



<p>Non-compliance can result in suspension or cancellation of your OVSE status. Beyond that, violations of the Aadhaar Act — such as storing full Aadhaar numbers, sharing data with third parties, or using verification data for purposes outside the declared scope — can attract penalties under the Act. This makes ongoing compliance, including regular audits and secure data handling, essential rather than optional.</p>



<p><strong>5. What is an Aadhaar Verifiable Credential (AVC) and how does it relate to OVSE?</strong> </p>



<p>The AVC is a digitally signed document issued by UIDAI that contains selective demographic data, including the last four digits of the Aadhaar number and a photograph, and is shared by the Aadhaar number holder with an OVSE — ensuring the holder maintains control over its dissemination.<a href="https://vinodkothari.com/2025/12/a-new-way-to-verify-aadhaar-offline-introduction-of-face-matching/" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" target="_blank"> Vinod Kothari Consultants</a> </p>



<p>Only registered OVSEs can accept and verify AVCs, which is one of the key reasons the December 2025 regulatory amendments make OVSE registration more valuable than ever.</p>



<p><strong>6. Do we have to build our own technical infrastructure to become an OVSE, or can we use a third-party provider?</strong> </p>



<p>You can use a certified third-party provider. Companies like Decentro hold OVSE certification and offer API-based access to compliant Aadhaar Offline KYC infrastructure. This means you can integrate offline verification into your product without independently registering with UIDAI or building the technical stack — including signature validation, consent workflows, and audit logging — from scratch. For most startups, this is the faster, lower-risk route.</p>
